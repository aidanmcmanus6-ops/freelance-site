If you've searched for what a website costs, you've probably found the same useless answer everywhere: "It depends — somewhere between $500 and $50,000." Technically true, completely unhelpful. Here's the honest 2026 breakdown for a small business website in South Jersey — what each option really costs, what drives the price up, and how to avoid both overpaying an agency and underpaying for something you'll have to rebuild in a year.

What actually drives the price

The single biggest factor isn't "design" — it's scope. A website's cost scales with how much there is to build and how custom it needs to be. The main drivers:

  • Number of pages. A 2-page launch site is a fraction of a 10-page site with a blog, services, and team pages.
  • Custom design vs. template. A unique, branded design costs more than styling a pre-built theme — but it's what makes you look like a real business instead of a template.
  • Content readiness. If your copy, photos, and logo are ready, the build moves fast. If someone has to write it, that's added time.
  • Functionality. A booking system, payment processing, a customer login, or integrations with your other tools each add real work.
  • E-commerce. Selling products online is a different tier — inventory, checkout, and payments add up quickly.

If you only remember one thing: a simple, well-built site that does one job well is worth more than a sprawling one nobody maintains.

Your three real options (and the honest trade-offs)

1. DIY builders — Wix, Squarespace, GoDaddy

Cost: $0–$500 up front, plus ~$200–$400/year. The trade-off: you do all the work, and you're renting your site — it lives on their platform, looks like a template, and is hard to move later. Fine for a side project or a temporary placeholder. For a business you want taken seriously, it usually shows.

2. An independent senior developer

Cost: roughly $1,000–$5,000. The trade-off: you get custom, professional work and direct access to the person actually building it — no account managers, no markup for a big office. The "risk" people worry about (a flaky freelancer) is real if you hire cheap; it disappears when you hire someone with a track record who owns the outcome end to end.

3. An agency or studio

Cost: $8,000–$30,000+. The trade-off: you get a full team and process — worth it for complex brands and big budgets. For a typical local small business, you're often paying for overhead (project managers, offices, sales teams) more than for the actual website.

The ongoing costs nobody mentions

The build is one number. Owning a website has a few recurring costs that catch people off guard:

  • Domain name: ~$12–$20/year.
  • Hosting: $0–$30/month depending on the setup (some modern sites host for free or near-free).
  • Maintenance: updates, content changes, security, and fixes. You can do this yourself, or put it on a retainer (mine start at $500/month) so it's handled.

Budget for these from the start — a site that's never updated slowly stops working in your favor.

How to avoid overpaying (and underpaying)

A few questions that cut through the noise when you're getting quotes:

  • "Will I own the site and the code?" If the answer is no, you're renting.
  • "Who actually builds it — and will I talk to them?" Layers between you and the builder usually mean markup and slower changes.
  • "What happens after launch?" A good build includes a clean handoff and a clear path for updates.
  • "Is it mobile-fast?" Most of your visitors are on phones, and Google ranks fast, mobile-friendly sites higher.

Underpaying (cheapest freelancer, abandoned DIY build) usually costs more in the long run — you end up paying twice when you rebuild. Overpaying an agency for a simple site is just lost margin. The sweet spot for most local businesses is a senior independent who builds it right the first time.

The bottom line

For a small business in South Jersey in 2026, a professional website typically runs $1,000–$5,000 with an independent developer — less if you DIY, much more with an agency. What you pay should track your scope, not someone's overhead.
